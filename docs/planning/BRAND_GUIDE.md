## 1. Brand Strategy

### 1.1 Brand Purpose

**Why does this brand exist?**

Most organizations know they need to improve. They invest in project management tools, hire consultants, attend workshops. But the tools they use separate *what needs to get done* from *how to think about what needs to get done.* Jira tracks tickets. Asana manages tasks. Neither one teaches a team how to properly define a problem, find the root cause, or measure whether their solution actually worked.

PIPS 2.0 exists to close that gap. It embeds a proven problem-solving methodology into the daily work of organizations so that improvement is not a separate initiative people attend a training for -- it is the way work gets done.

**Purpose statement:** *To make structured problem-solving and continuous improvement the default operating system for how organizations work.*

### 1.2 Brand Vision

**What does PIPS 2.0 aspire to be?**

The standard that organizations reach for when they say "we want to get better." Not a tool they use alongside their methodology, but the place where methodology and work management are the same thing. In five years, when a VP of Operations says "we need to fix this," the natural next step is opening PIPS 2.0 and walking the team through a structured process -- not scheduling a meeting, not opening a spreadsheet, not hiring a consultant.

**Vision statement:** *Every organization has a repeatable, measurable system for solving problems and improving processes -- and it lives where their teams already work.*

### 1.3 Brand Mission

**What does PIPS 2.0 do every day?**

PIPS 2.0 gives teams a structured 6-step workflow for process improvement and problem solving, embedded in enterprise-grade project management software. It guides teams from problem identification through root cause analysis, solution design, implementation, and measurable evaluation -- while also handling the everyday tickets, tasks, and projects that result from that process.

**Mission statement:** *We give organizations the methodology and the tools to identify, solve, and measure their most important problems -- in one platform.*

### 1.4 Brand Values

**Five core values that govern every decision:**

#### 1. Structured Clarity
Ambiguity kills improvement projects. Every feature, every screen, every workflow in PIPS 2.0 reduces ambiguity. We believe that a well-defined problem is half-solved, and our product reflects that belief in how it guides users. Our brand communication is similarly direct -- no jargon, no buzzword soup, no vague promises.

#### 2. Measurable Impact
If you cannot measure it, you cannot improve it. We are obsessed with outcomes, not activity. Our product measures results. Our marketing cites real numbers. Our customers know exactly what they are getting and can prove it worked.

#### 3. Accessible Rigor
The best methodologies in the world are useless if only experts can apply them. PIPS 2.0 takes the rigor of Six Sigma, Lean, and PDCA and makes it accessible to any team -- without dumbing it down. Our brand strikes the same balance: professional and substantive, but never intimidating or exclusive.

#### 4. Continuous Evolution
Step 6 feeds back to Step 1. This is not just a product feature; it is a philosophy. We improve our own product, our own processes, and our own brand the same way we teach others to. Nothing is static. Everything is iterable.

#### 5. Earned Trust
We serve operations leaders, strategy executives, and consultants who manage high-stakes initiatives. Trust is the price of entry. We earn it through transparency, reliability, data privacy, and following through on what we promise. Our brand projects confidence, not hype.

### 1.5 Brand Personality

**If PIPS 2.0 were a person, who would they be?**

A senior operations executive who also happens to be a great teacher. They have deep experience -- they have run improvement programs at multiple organizations across industries. They are calm under pressure, data-driven, and precise in their language. But they are not cold or corporate. They have a gift for explaining complex frameworks in plain language. They make people feel capable, not inadequate. They dress well -- modern, clean, tailored -- but not flashy. They are the person in the room who brings structure to chaos without making anyone feel talked down to.

**Personality traits:**
- **Authoritative** but not arrogant
- **Precise** but not rigid
- **Warm** but not casual
- **Modern** but not trendy
- **Ambitious** but not reckless

### 1.6 Brand Archetype

**Primary: The Sage**
The Sage archetype seeks truth, understanding, and knowledge. PIPS 2.0 is fundamentally about helping organizations understand their problems more deeply and solve them more effectively. The Sage shares knowledge, provides frameworks, and illuminates the path forward.

**Secondary: The Hero**
The Hero archetype proves worth through courageous action. PIPS 2.0 empowers teams to take on their hardest operational challenges with a structured approach. The product does not solve problems for you -- it gives you the framework and tools to be the hero of your own improvement story.

**Why not The Explorer or The Creator?**
- The Explorer suggests open-ended discovery, which conflicts with our structured, methodology-driven approach.
- The Creator suggests building from nothing, when PIPS 2.0 is really about disciplined improvement of what already exists.

### 1.7 Brand Voice Attributes

**How PIPS 2.0 speaks:**

| Attribute | What it sounds like | What it does NOT sound like |
|-----------|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| **Direct** | "Define your problem in one measurable statement." | "Consider exploring the various dimensions of your challenge space." |
| **Substantive** | "Teams using structured root cause analysis resolve issues 2.3x faster." | "Supercharge your problem-solving! 10x your results!" |
| **Guiding** | "Start here. Identify who is affected and what success looks like." | "Figure out what your problem is and enter it." |
| **Confident** | "This is a proven 6-step approach." | "We think this might work for you." |
| **Human** | "Every organization has that one process that everyone complains about but nobody fixes. Start there." | "Leverage our platform to optimize your organizational throughput vectors." |
| **Inclusive** | "Whether you run a hospital, a factory, or a software company." | "For enterprise-class operational excellence professionals." |

**Tone calibration by context:**

- **Marketing website:** Confident + Human. Lead with outcomes, speak in the second person ("you"), use concrete examples.
- **Product UI:** Direct + Guiding. Short, action-oriented copy. Tell users what to do next.
- **Documentation:** Substantive + Inclusive. Thorough, well-organized, assumes intelligence but not prior knowledge.
- **Sales materials:** Confident + Substantive. Data-driven, ROI-focused, respectful of buyer's time.
- **Error messages:** Human + Direct. Acknowledge the problem, explain what happened, tell them what to do.

### 1.8 Brand Promise

**What PIPS 2.0 guarantees:**

*A clear, structured path from problem to solution -- and the tools to manage every step of the journey.*

This promise has three components:
1. **Clarity:** You will never wonder "what should we do next?" The methodology guides you.
2. **Structure:** The 6-step framework is not a suggestion. It is built into the product.
3. **Completeness:** This is not just a methodology tool or just a project management tool. It is both.

## 2. Naming

### 2.1 Full Name Evaluation

#### Option 1: PIPS (Keep the current name)

**Full name:** PIPS
**Stands for:** Process Improvement and Problem Solving
**Domain plausibility:** pips.com is almost certainly taken (it is a common English word). pips.io is more plausible but likely parked. getpips.com, usepips.com, or pipsapp.com are more realistic targets.

**In context:**
- "Welcome to PIPS" -- fine, casual
- "Powered by PIPS" -- works, but feels small
- "PIPS Enterprise" -- awkward; sounds like a character name getting a promotion

**Pros:**
- Short, memorable, easy to spell and say
- Existing recognition within RxLogic and early ForgePIPS users
- The acronym is meaningful and relevant
- Already has a deployed product (ForgePIPS)

**Cons:**
- Heavy namespace collision: forex pips (very common in finance search results), Python pip, Gladys Knight & the Pips, the word "pips" (as in seeds, card suit marks, radar blips)
- SEO will be difficult. Searching "PIPS project management" returns mixed results dominated by other meanings
- Sounds diminutive. "Pips" has a small, cute connotation (think: the small dots on dice). This clashes with enterprise positioning
- Does not convey project management, strategy, or execution
- Four letters that do not visually suggest technology or sophistication
- White-label clients may hesitate to associate their brand with a name that sounds informal

### 2.3 Final Recommendation: Meridian

**The recommended name for PIPS 2.0 is Meridian.**

Here is the reasoning:

**1. Enterprise credibility from day one.** When a VP of Operations at a Fortune 500 company hears "We use Meridian for our improvement projects," it sounds like a serious platform. When they hear "We use PIPS," it sounds like an internal initiative someone named. The naming bar for enterprise SaaS is high, and Meridian clears it effortlessly.

**2. The metaphor is deep and relevant.** Meridians are the structural framework that makes navigation possible. That is exactly what this product does -- it provides the structural framework (the 6-step methodology) that makes organizational improvement navigable. Meridians are also circular, echoing the iterative cycle that is central to the PIPS methodology. And "meridian" means the highest point -- the peak -- which is aspirational without being hyperbolic.

**3. White-label strength.** "Powered by Meridian" is one of the strongest-sounding white-label badges of all six options. It sounds like premium infrastructure that a client would be proud to have under their brand.

**4. Visual identity potential.** Meridian lines, compass roses, circular arcs, and navigational imagery provide a rich visual vocabulary that will serve the brand well across logo design, iconography, data visualization, and UI elements.

**5. Practical considerations.** The word is familiar, easy to spell, easy to pronounce, and works in all major languages. It has positive connotations universally. While domain acquisition will require creativity (meridianpm.com, getmeridian.com, meridian.io), this is a solvable problem.

**6. The PIPS methodology lives inside Meridian.** PIPS does not disappear. "The PIPS Methodology" becomes the named framework inside the Meridian platform, similar to how Salesforce has its "Lightning Experience" or how Agile has Scrum. The product is Meridian; the methodology is PIPS. This gives you two brandable assets instead of one.

**Recommended structure:**
- **Company/Platform:** Meridian
- **Methodology:** The PIPS Framework (or PIPS Methodology)
- **Tagline:** *Structured improvement. Measurable results.*
- **Marketing usage:** "Meridian is powered by the PIPS Methodology -- a proven 6-step framework for process improvement and problem solving."

### 2.4 Name Usage Rules

**The product name:**
- Full name: **Meridian**
- NEVER: "The Meridian," "MERIDIAN" (all caps unless in a logo lockup), "Meridian.io" (do not include the TLD in text)
- In body text, always capitalize: "Meridian" (not "meridian")
- First reference in any document: "Meridian, the methodology-driven project management platform" (or similar descriptor)
- Subsequent references: just "Meridian"

**The methodology name:**
- Full name: **The PIPS Framework** or **The PIPS Methodology**
- ALWAYS capitalize PIPS (it is an acronym)
- First reference in context: "The PIPS Framework (Process Improvement and Problem Solving)"
- Subsequent references: "PIPS" or "the PIPS process"
- The six steps are always referenced by number and name: "Step 1: Identify," "Step 2: Analyze," etc.

### 2.5 Sub-Brand Naming Convention

**Product tiers:**
- Meridian Starter
- Meridian Professional
- Meridian Enterprise
- Meridian Unlimited

**Feature modules:**
Use functional, descriptive names (not branded sub-names):
- Meridian Ticketing
- Meridian Analytics
- Meridian Integrations
- Meridian API

**The PIPS steps within the product:**
Always use the step number + verb:
- Step 1: Identify
- Step 2: Analyze
- Step 3: Generate
- Step 4: Select & Plan
- Step 5: Implement
- Step 6: Evaluate

These steps are NOT given separate brand names. They are always referred to by their methodology names.

### 2.6 How the Name Works with White-Labeling

When a client white-labels Meridian:
- The client's brand replaces "Meridian" everywhere in the UI
- "Powered by Meridian" appears in the footer (small, tasteful)
- The PIPS Framework methodology naming remains intact (it is the methodology, not the brand)
- Clients may optionally rename "PIPS" to their own methodology name at the Enterprise tier
- The 6 step names (Identify, Analyze, Generate, Select & Plan, Implement, Evaluate) are configurable at Enterprise tier

#### Color Usage Ratios

The Meridian palette follows a 60-30-10 distribution:

- **60% Neutrals:** Page backgrounds, card backgrounds, text. This is the canvas.
- **30% Primary (Navy + Blue):** Headers, navigation, key structural elements. This is the frame.
- **10% Accent (Cyan + Step colors + Semantic colors):** CTAs, active states, indicators, badges. This is the signal.

Never use accent colors for large background areas. Never use step colors as primary brand colors outside of step-specific contexts.

#### Accessibility Notes (WCAG AA)

The following pairs meet WCAG AA contrast requirements (4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text):

**Light mode:**
- Neutral 700 (`#334155`) on Neutral 50 (`#F8FAFC`) -- 10.4:1 (primary text)
- Neutral 500 (`#64748B`) on Neutral 50 (`#F8FAFC`) -- 5.0:1 (secondary text)
- White (`#FFFFFF`) on Meridian Blue (`#1E40AF`) -- 7.2:1 (buttons)
- White (`#FFFFFF`) on Meridian Cyan (`#06B6D4`) -- 3.4:1 (large text only; use Navy text for small cyan elements)
- Neutral 50 (`#F8FAFC`) on Meridian Navy (`#0F1B3D`) -- 14.5:1 (dark headers)

**Dark mode:**
- Neutral 200 (`#E2E8F0`) on Neutral 900 (`#0F172A`) -- 12.7:1 (primary text)
- Neutral 400 (`#94A3B8`) on Neutral 900 (`#0F172A`) -- 5.4:1 (secondary text)
- White on Meridian Blue (`#1E40AF`) -- 7.2:1 (buttons)

**Critical note on cyan:** Meridian Cyan (`#06B6D4`) does NOT meet WCAG AA for normal-weight text on white backgrounds. When using cyan for text, always use a heavier weight (600+) or larger size (18px+), or darken to Meridian Teal (`#0D9488`) which achieves 4.6:1 on white.

#### White-Label Color Adaptation

When clients white-label the platform:
- **Primary colors** (Navy, Blue, Cyan) are replaced by the client's brand colors
- **Neutral palette** remains fixed (it is structural, not branded)
- **Semantic colors** remain fixed (success/warning/error are universal)
- **Step colors** can be customized at the Enterprise tier. At lower tiers, they remain the Meridian defaults. If customized, the system validates WCAG contrast ratios automatically.
- The CSS custom property system (see Section 8) makes this swappable with a single theme file

### 3.2 Typography

#### Primary Typeface: Inter

**Headlines, labels, navigation, buttons.**

[Inter](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Inter) is a purpose-built UI typeface designed for computer screens. It has excellent legibility at all sizes, a large x-height, and a professional, modern feel. It was designed by Rasmus Andersson and is used by GitHub, Figma, and Linear -- the caliber of design-forward software Meridian aspires to sit alongside.

**Weights used:**
- **Inter 400 (Regular):** Body text in some contexts, form labels
- **Inter 500 (Medium):** Navigation items, secondary buttons, table headers
- **Inter 600 (SemiBold):** Subheadings (H3-H6), button text, emphasis
- **Inter 700 (Bold):** Page headings (H1-H2), hero text, key statistics
- **Inter 800 (ExtraBold):** Display text (marketing pages only), hero headlines

#### Secondary Typeface: Inter

**Body text, paragraphs, descriptions.**

Inter is used for both headlines and body text. A single-font system reduces load time, simplifies the design system, and ensures visual consistency. The variation comes from weight, size, and color -- not from font switching.

**Why not a serif or a second sans-serif?**
- Serif fonts (like Merriweather or Lora) add warmth but introduce complexity in a product UI context
- A second sans-serif (like Source Sans Pro) would be redundant alongside Inter
- Single-font systems are easier to maintain, faster to load, and produce more cohesive designs
- If a warmer or more editorial feel is needed for long-form content (blog, case studies), consider adding **Source Serif 4** (400, 600) as a supplementary typeface

#### Monospace Typeface: JetBrains Mono

**Code blocks, API documentation, data tables with IDs, technical content.**

[JetBrains Mono](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/JetBrains+Mono) has ligatures for common code sequences, excellent readability at small sizes, and a modern feel that matches Inter's aesthetic.

**Weights used:**
- **JetBrains Mono 400 (Regular):** Code blocks, inline code
- **JetBrains Mono 500 (Medium):** Code with emphasis, terminal commands

#### Line Heights

- Headlines (Display, H1-H3): tight (1.1-1.3) for visual impact
- Body text: relaxed (1.5-1.6) for readability in paragraphs

- Large text (Display, H1-H3): negative tracking (-0.015em to -0.025em) to tighten headlines

- Small text and overlines: positive tracking (0.01em to 0.08em) to improve legibility at small sizes

#### PIPS Step Visual Differentiation in the UI

The 6 PIPS steps are differentiated across the product through a consistent, multi-layered system:

4. **Step Cards:** In board/kanban views, each column corresponds to a step and has the step color as a subtle header accent (8px colored bar at top, or left border).

5. **Step Sidebar:** In the project detail view, a vertical step list on the left shows all 6 steps with their icons, names, and completion status. The current step is highlighted with its color.

### 4.3 Animation & Motion

#### Transition Standards

| Property | Duration | Easing | Usage |
|----------|----------|--------|-------|
| Color, background, border | 150ms | ease | Hover states, focus states |
| Opacity | 200ms | ease-out | Fade in/out, appear/disappear |
| Transform (scale, translate) | 200ms | cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1) | Slide in, pop, scale |
| Width, height, max-height | 250ms | ease-in-out | Expand/collapse, accordion |
| Box-shadow | 150ms | ease | Card hover elevation |

**CSS custom properties:**
```css
:root {
  --duration-fast: 100ms;
  --duration-normal: 150ms;
  --duration-slow: 250ms;
  --duration-slower: 350ms;
  --ease-default: ease;
  --ease-in-out: ease-in-out;
  --ease-spring: cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1);
  --ease-bounce: cubic-bezier(0.34, 1.56, 0.64, 1);
}
```

#### Micro-Interaction Patterns

**Button press:** `scale(0.97)` on `:active`, 100ms, ease. Returns to `scale(1)` on release.

**Checkbox/toggle:** Toggle slides from left to right with a 200ms spring ease. The track color transitions from Neutral 300 to Meridian Blue.

**Dropdown open:** Opacity 0 to 1, translateY(-4px) to translateY(0), 200ms, spring ease. Close is the reverse at 150ms.

**Toast notification:** Slides in from bottom-right, translateY(16px) to translateY(0), opacity 0 to 1, 300ms. Auto-dismisses after 5 seconds with a fade-out (200ms).

**Step progress advance:** The active node scales up (1x to 1.2x) and the connector line fills with color left-to-right over 300ms. The previous node's scale returns to 1x and a checkmark fades in.

#### Loading States

**Skeleton screens** are the primary loading pattern (not spinners):
- Replace content areas with animated placeholder blocks
- Blocks are Neutral 200 (light) / Neutral 700 (dark) with a shimmer animation
- Shimmer: a subtle left-to-right gradient sweep, repeating every 1.5 seconds
- Match the expected layout structure (don't show a generic spinner -- show the skeleton of what's about to appear)

**Spinners** are used only for:
- Small inline loading (saving a form, sending a request): 16px spinner, Meridian Cyan
- Modal/dialog loading: 24px spinner centered, with "Loading..." text below
- Full-page initial load: The PIPS cycle mark animating (each of the 6 segments lighting up in sequence)

#### Page Transitions

For a web application, keep page transitions minimal:
- **Route change:** Content area fades (opacity 0 to 1, 150ms). No sliding or scaling.
- **Tab switch (within a page):** Content area cross-fades (outgoing opacity 1 to 0, incoming 0 to 1, 200ms overlap).
- **Modal open:** Scrim fades in (opacity 0 to 0.5, 200ms), modal slides up (translateY(8px) to 0, 250ms, spring ease).
- **Sidebar collapse:** Width animates from 260px to 60px over 250ms, ease-in-out. Content area expands to fill the space.

**Motion philosophy:** Motion should be fast, functional, and nearly invisible. The user should feel that things are responsive, not that they are watching animations. If a user notices the animation, it is too long or too dramatic.

## 6. Brand Guidelines Summary

### 6.1 The "Do" and "Don't" List

#### DO:

- Use the Meridian name consistently with a capital M
- Refer to the methodology as "The PIPS Framework" or "The PIPS Methodology"
- Use step colors only in step-specific contexts
- Maintain adequate contrast ratios (WCAG AA minimum)
- Use Inter for all text in the product and marketing materials
- Lead with outcomes and benefits in marketing copy
- Use the Step Gradient Stripe as a signature brand element
- Keep illustrations abstract and geometric
- Use skeleton screens for loading states
- Let whitespace breathe -- do not cram content into every pixel
- Test all color combinations for accessibility before shipping

#### DON'T:

- Never write "MERIDIAN" in all caps in body text (only in logo lockups)
- Never use "The Meridian" with a definite article
- Never stretch, rotate, or distort the logo
- Never use step colors as primary brand colors outside of step contexts
- Never use Meridian Cyan for small body text on white backgrounds (contrast fails)
- Never use pie charts, 3D charts, or radar charts in data visualizations
- Never use stock photos as hero backgrounds
- Never use more than 2 step colors in a single UI element
- Never use animation durations longer than 350ms for UI interactions
- Never use custom fonts beyond Inter and JetBrains Mono in the product
- Never remove the "Powered by Meridian" badge from white-label instances at tiers below Enterprise
- Never use the word "synergy," "leverage" (as a verb), "ideate," or "paradigm" in brand communications
- Never place the logo smaller than its minimum size (120px for full logo, 24px for icon)
- Never use a colored background behind the full-color logo (use the monochrome version)

### 6.2 Common Misuse Examples

| Misuse | Problem | Correct Usage |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| "Welcome to the Meridian" | Definite article not used with the name | "Welcome to Meridian" |
| "MERIDIAN helps teams..." | All-caps in body text | "Meridian helps teams..." |
| Using Step 5 Coral as a button color | Step colors are not UI action colors | Use Meridian Blue for buttons |
| Cyan text on white background at 14px | Fails WCAG AA contrast | Use Meridian Blue or Teal for small text |
| Rainbow gradient using all 6 step colors in a card | Overuse of step colors | Use one step color per context |
| "Powered by PIPS" in white-label | PIPS is the methodology, Meridian is the platform | "Powered by Meridian" |
| Using a spinner for page load | Inconsistent with brand motion patterns | Use skeleton screens |
| Logo on a busy photo background | Reduces legibility | Logo on solid or near-solid backgrounds only |

- Typography scale (sizes, line heights, spacing ratios)

- Icon library (Lucide base set)

- PIPS methodology content and logic (the 6-step framework itself)

### 7.3 Theming Architecture

White-label theming is implemented entirely through CSS custom properties (design tokens). The architecture:

```
Base tokens (Meridian defaults)
  ├── Overridden by: Organization theme (stored in database)
  │     ├── Primary color → generates derived colors automatically
  │     ├── Accent color → generates derived colors automatically
  │     ├── Logo URL
  │     └── Optional overrides (step colors, font, etc.)
  └── Applied via: CSS custom property injection at page load
```

**Technical implementation:**

1. On login, the server looks up the organization's theme settings from the database
2. A `<style>` tag is injected into the `<head>` that overrides the default CSS custom properties
3. All components reference tokens, never hard-coded colors
4. The system auto-generates derived colors (hover states, focus rings, text-on-color) from the primary and accent colors using HSL manipulation

**Example theme override:**

```css
/* Client theme: "Acme Corp" */
:root {
  --color-primary: #7C3AED;           /* Acme's purple */
  --color-primary-hover: #6D28D9;
  --color-primary-light: #A78BFA;
  --color-primary-subtle: rgba(124, 58, 237, 0.1);
  --color-accent: #F59E0B;            /* Acme's gold */
  --color-accent-hover: #D97706;
  --font-brand: 'Poppins', var(--font-sans);  /* Enterprise tier only */
}
```

### 7.4 Step Color Adaptation

When Enterprise clients customize the 6 step colors:

1. **Validation:** The system checks each custom color for WCAG AA contrast against both the light and dark background colors. If a color fails, the system suggests a corrected version.
2. **Derived colors:** For each step color, the system automatically generates:
   - A 15% opacity background variant (for step cards)
   - A dark-mode brightened variant
   - A text-safe variant (darkened if necessary for text usage)
3. **Fallback:** If a client provides only a partial step color set (e.g., only changes 3 of 6 colors), the remaining steps use the Meridian defaults.
4. **Preview:** The admin settings page includes a live preview of how the custom step colors look in the step progress bar, step cards, and data visualizations before saving.

### 7.5 "Powered by Meridian" Badge

**Design:**
- Text: "Powered by Meridian" in Inter 400, 11px, Neutral 400
- Optional: Small Meridian Arc icon (12px) to the left of the text
- Placement: Footer of the application, right-aligned
- Minimum margin: 16px from any other element
- Link: Clicks through to the Meridian marketing site

**Tier behavior:**
- Starter / Professional: Badge is always present. Can be recolored to match client theme but not hidden.
- Enterprise: Badge can be removed entirely as part of the license agreement.

**Design constraints:**
- The badge must never be overlaid on a busy background
- Minimum contrast of 3:1 against its background
- Never modify the badge text (no "Built with Meridian" or "Running on Meridian")

## Appendix: Design Decision Log

| Decision | Choice | Rationale |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| Name | Meridian (not PIPS) | Enterprise credibility, metaphor depth, white-label strength |
| Primary font | Inter (single-font system) | Consistency, performance, proven in top SaaS products |
| Monospace font | JetBrains Mono | Modern, ligatures, matches Inter aesthetic |
| Primary brand color | Navy + Blue (not cyan-forward) | Enterprise gravity; cyan serves as accent |
| Step colors | 6 distinct, harmonious hues | Must differentiate in all contexts (color-blind safe when combined with numbers/icons) |
| Icon style | Duotone (Lucide base) | Richer than outline, less heavy than filled; balances professional and modern |
| Border radius | 8px default | Rounded but not bubbly; professional without being cold |
| Navigation | Left sidebar | Complex product with many sections; top nav would be too constraining |
| Loading pattern | Skeleton screens | Better perceived performance, matches modern SaaS standard |
| Illustration style | Abstract geometric | Scalable, on-brand, culturally neutral, cheap to produce |
| Dark mode approach | CSS custom property overrides | Single codebase, respects system preference, easy to maintain |
| White-label architecture | CSS tokens + DB-stored overrides | Maximum flexibility with minimum code complexity |
